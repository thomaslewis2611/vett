The average UK buyer spends around **4 minutes** reading a Rightmove listing before deciding whether to book a viewing. The average UK property costs £285,000. That works out to about £1,187 of due diligence per second.

This guide covers what to actually look for — and why the things agents highlight are often the least important things on the page.

## Start with what isn't there

The most useful information on any Rightmove listing is what the agent chose not to include.

**No EPC rating?** Either it's missing (a legal obligation for marketed properties, with limited exceptions), or it's so poor the agent is hoping you won't ask. An EPC rating of E, F, or G can mean £3,000–£5,000 in annual energy costs on a mid-sized property, and from 2028 rental EPC minimums will tighten significantly.

**No floor plan?** Floor plans reveal the actual room sizes and layout. Without one, a "spacious" double bedroom might be 8ft × 9ft — technically a double, functionally a box room. Always request the floor plan before booking a viewing.

**Price reduced?** Rightmove shows price history. A property that's been reduced by 5–10% after sitting on market for 60+ days tells you the original asking price was aspirational, and there may be more room to negotiate.

## The pricing question

Every buyer wants to know: is this property fairly priced?

The most reliable benchmark is **sold price per square foot** for comparable properties within 0.25 miles, sold in the last 18 months. This filters out property-type differences (detached vs flat) and gives you a like-for-like comparison.

Here's what the numbers typically look like:

| Property type | Area average £/sqft | What it means |
|--------------|---------------------|---------------|
| This property at £550/sqft | Area average £490/sqft | 12% above — needs justification |
| This property at £420/sqft | Area average £490/sqft | 14% below — potentially good value |

The justification for above-average pricing might be: south-facing garden, recent extension, parking in a permit area, or proximity to an outstanding school. The question is whether the premium is proportionate.

Without the square footage, this analysis is impossible. If the listing doesn't state it, check if the EPC certificate shows floor area (it usually does for properties that have been rented or recently marketed).

## Red flags checklist

Run through these before every viewing:

### Legal and structural
- **Leasehold?** Check the remaining lease length. Below 80 years, mortgage lenders get uncomfortable and extension costs rise sharply. Below 70 years, some lenders won't lend at all.
- **Service charge and ground rent?** For flats, ask for the last 3 years of service charge accounts. A building with deferred maintenance is a building with a large special levy coming.
- **Listed building?** Grade I or II listing means significant restrictions on alterations, extensions, and even repairs. Insurance is typically 20–40% more expensive.
- **Conservation area?** Similar restrictions to listed, affecting extensions, window replacements, and permitted development rights.

### Physical condition
- **Property description mentions damp, plaster repairs, or "potential"?** These are soft signals that something needs attention. "Potential" in an agent description almost always means "currently in poor condition."
- **Kitchen and bathroom age?** A 2006 kitchen in a 2006 flat isn't a selling point — it's a 20-year-old kitchen due for replacement at £15,000–£30,000.
- **Photo angles?** Agents shoot wide-angle. A kitchen that looks spacious might be 7ft wide. Count the windows in photos and cross-reference with the floor plan.

### Location
- **Flood risk?** The Environment Agency's flood risk maps are free and public. Any property with a medium or high flood risk will face higher insurance premiums, potential mortgage issues, and resale difficulty.
- **Transport links?** For London properties specifically, check the PTAL (Public Transport Accessibility Level) score. PTAL 1 means 30+ minute walk to public transport; PTAL 6 means you're on top of it.
- **Planning applications nearby?** Check the local council's planning portal for applications within 200 metres. A supermarket, block of flats, or industrial unit approved nearby will affect both your experience of the property and its future value.

## The EPC analysis

The Energy Performance Certificate is one of the most underused pieces of data in a property purchase.

Beyond the headline rating (A–G), the EPC shows:
- **Estimated annual energy costs** — often significantly underestimated, but a useful baseline
- **Recommended improvements** — the surveyor's view of what would make the most impact
- **Potential rating** — what the property could achieve with the recommended improvements

A property rated EPC D with potential EPC B is telling you that insulation and a heat pump could transform its running costs. A property rated EPC E with potential EPC E is telling you the building's structure limits how much improvement is possible.

For first-time buyers, the EPC is also relevant to mortgage affordability calculations, as some lenders now offer better rates for energy-efficient properties.

## Making sense of the listing description

Estate agent language has a dialect of its own. A brief translation guide:

| Agent says | Often means |
|-----------|-------------|
| "Full of character" | Original (i.e. draughty) windows, uneven floors |
| "Ideal first-time buyer property" | Small, possibly needs work |
| "Investment opportunity" | Needs significant renovation |
| "Chain-free" | Might mean the seller is motivated; might mean probate |
| "Offers in excess of" | They want more than the stated price |
| "Guide price" | Could be an auction property — check carefully |
| "Scope for extension" (STPP) | Subject to planning permission, which isn't guaranteed |

## Before you make an offer

Before committing to a viewing, let alone an offer, you should know:

1. **The area average £/sqft** for this property type in this postcode
2. **Whether the flood risk is low, medium, or high**
3. **The nearest schools and their Ofsted ratings**
4. **The EPC rating and estimated annual energy costs**
5. **How long it's been on market** and whether the price has been reduced

Most of this information is publicly available, scattered across different sources: the Land Registry, the Environment Agency, Ofsted, Ofcom's connected nations report. The problem isn't that it doesn't exist — it's that gathering it manually takes hours most buyers don't have.
