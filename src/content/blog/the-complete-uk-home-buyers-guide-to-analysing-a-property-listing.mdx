*How to cut through estate agent spin, spot red flags, and make a smarter offer — before you even step through the door.*

## Why most buyers are flying blind

Buying a home is almost certainly the biggest financial decision you will ever make. Yet most buyers walk into viewings armed with little more than a listing description written by someone whose sole job is to sell you the property as quickly as possible, for as much as possible.

That is not a conspiracy theory — it is just how the market works. Estate agents are paid by sellers. Their listing descriptions are legal documents, yes, but they are also carefully crafted marketing copy, designed to emphasise the positive and gloss over the difficult. Words are chosen deliberately. Details are omitted strategically.

The result? Buyers fall in love at the viewing, make offers based on emotion, and only discover the problems — the service charge, the noisy road, the structural survey bill — after they have committed. By then, pulling out costs time, money, and a great deal of stress.

This guide gives you what experienced buyers know: how to read a listing properly, what the jargon actually means, which red flags to look for in photos and floorplans, how to judge whether a price is fair, and exactly what questions to ask at a viewing. By the end, you will approach property listings the way a seasoned buyer does — sceptically, systematically, and with your eyes wide open.

## Section 1: How property listings are written to deceive

Let us be clear: estate agents are not lying. What they are doing is far more subtle — and often more effective.

### The language of strategic omission

A listing description for a property is a sales document, not an information document. Its job is to generate viewings, not to give you a balanced assessment of the property's merits and flaws. Understanding this distinction is the first step to reading listings properly.

Here are the most common phrases you will encounter, and what they frequently signal in practice:

- **"Deceptively spacious"** — The property looks smaller than it is from the outside. This sounds like a positive, and sometimes it is. But it is also frequently used when internal layouts are awkward, ceilings are low, or the floor space is oddly distributed.
- **"In need of some modernisation"** — This is agent-speak for "needs significant work." At minimum, expect dated kitchens and bathrooms. At worst, expect structural issues, old wiring, or a full renovation project.
- **"Bijou" or "characterful"** — Small. These words are almost always used to soften the blow of limited square footage.
- **"Investment opportunity"** — Typically means the property requires substantial work and would not be mortgageable in its current state.
- **"Easy to maintain garden"** — The garden is very small, paved over, or both.
- **"Well-connected location" or "vibrant neighbourhood"** — May indicate proximity to a busy road, train line, or an area with noise or antisocial behaviour concerns. Always check the map.
- **"Viewing recommended"** — Almost meaningless as a phrase, but worth noting when the photos are suspiciously sparse or the description is vague. The agent knows something looks better in person than online.

### What listing descriptions frequently leave out

Just as important as what is said is what is not said. A listing will almost never voluntarily tell you:

- That the property is leasehold with a short lease or high service charges
- That it is above a commercial premises (which affects mortgageability)
- That there is an article 4 direction restricting permitted development rights
- That the property has previously flooded, or is in a flood risk zone
- That there is a major road or flight path nearby
- That the boiler, roof, or electrics are at end of life
- That the property has had subsidence, damp, or Japanese knotweed issues in the past

None of these omissions are necessarily dishonest — agents are not legally obliged to disclose most of them unprompted. But they are things you need to know before making an offer. The best defence is knowing what questions to ask.

## Section 2: How to read between the lines of a listing

Now you understand the game, here is how to play it better.

### Start with the price per square foot

Before you read a single word of the description, establish the price per square foot. Divide the asking price by the total square footage (usually shown in the floorplan). Then compare it against similar properties sold recently in the same street or postcode using Rightmove's sold prices tool or the Land Registry.

This single number cuts through a huge amount of noise. A property described as 'spacious' that comes in at significantly above the local price per square foot deserves much more scrutiny than its listing suggests.

### Read the description in reverse

Agents front-load listings with the best features. The things they are least enthusiastic about — if they mention them at all — tend to appear at the end, buried in the final paragraph or tucked into a legal footnote. Always read the last paragraph of a listing carefully.

### Count the rooms against the photos

If the listing claims five rooms but the photos only show three, the missing rooms are not being shown for a reason. Either they are too small to photograph well, they are in poor condition, or they face a direction the agent would rather you did not think about.

### Check what direction the property faces

A south-facing garden is worth real money in the UK. A north-facing one can mean a cold, dark property with limited natural light. Many listings omit this entirely. Use Google Maps satellite view to check orientation before you book a viewing — it takes 30 seconds and can save you hours.

### Look at how long it has been on the market

Rightmove and Zoopla both show the first listing date. A property that has been sitting on the market for more than 8 to 12 weeks in a normally active area is telling you something. Either it is overpriced, there is something putting buyers off, or previous sales have fallen through. All of these warrant investigation.

### Check the sold price history

The Land Registry shows every sale of a registered property going back decades. If a property sold 18 months ago for significantly less than the current asking price, and nothing major appears to have been done to it, ask why. If it sold multiple times in recent years, ask why buyers keep leaving.

## Section 3: Red flags in photos, floorplans, and descriptions

### Red flags in listing photos

Photos are carefully curated. Estate agents use wide-angle lenses, shoot in bright conditions, and select the most flattering angles. Here is what to look for despite this:

- **Furniture placed to obscure walls or floors.** A sofa pushed into a corner, a rug covering a large portion of the floor, or a wardrobe conveniently covering a section of wall — these are worth noting.
- **No photo of a specific room.** If the listing mentions a study or utility room but shows no photo of it, assume the worst until you see it in person.
- **Ceiling stains or patches.** Even with careful editing, water damage stains sometimes appear in corners of ceiling photos. Look carefully.
- **Condensation or mould around windows.** Check window frames and corners in bathroom and kitchen photos particularly.
- **Low photo count overall.** A four-bedroom house with six photos is hiding something. Modern listings for well-presented properties typically include 15 to 25 images.
- **External photos taken from unusual angles.** A front elevation shot taken from an extreme angle often means the agent is avoiding showing something directly in front of the property — a busy road, a car park, or a neighbouring eyesore.

### Red flags in floorplans

Floorplans are required for most listings now and are one of the most honest sources of information, because they are harder to manipulate than photos. Look for:

- **Rooms with no windows marked.** A bedroom with no window is either illegal under building regulations or is being described as a bedroom when it technically is not one.
- **Awkward layouts.** A bathroom only accessible through a bedroom, a kitchen you must walk through to reach the garden, or a living room that doubles as a corridor are all livability issues that agents rarely highlight.
- **Missing measurements.** When individual room sizes are omitted from the floorplan, it sometimes means rooms are smaller than they appear in photos.
- **Total square footage not shown.** Reputable agents include total square footage. When it is absent, calculate it yourself from the room dimensions shown — and compare to what you would expect for the price.

### Red flags in the description text

- Vague or absent tenure information — is it freehold, leasehold, or share of freehold? If it is not stated clearly, ask immediately.
- No mention of the boiler age or service history.
- Unusual or excessive legal disclaimers at the bottom of the listing.
- Chain information absent — a vendor who has not found onward is a sale that is more likely to fall through.
- References to "planning potential" without specifics — this often means the current structure has issues or limitations.

## Section 4: How to assess whether a price is fair

Asking prices in the UK are not neutral starting points — they are negotiating positions. Understanding true value before you make an offer is one of the most important things you can do.

### Use sold prices, not asking prices

The Rightmove and Land Registry sold prices tools show what properties in the same postcode actually sold for, not what they were asking. An agent can list a property at any price they agree with a vendor. What buyers actually paid is a much more honest data point.

### Adjust for differences

When comparing sold prices, adjust mentally (or with a simple calculation) for:

- Floor — ground floor flats command less than upper floors in most markets.
- Condition — a fully modernised property is worth more than one needing work; estimate renovation costs and subtract them from comparable sold prices.
- Garden size and orientation — south-facing gardens in good condition add real value.
- Parking — off-street parking in many areas adds several thousand pounds to market value.
- Tenure — leasehold properties with short leases or high service charges are worth materially less than their freehold equivalents.

### Calculate the true cost of ownership

The asking price is just the beginning. Before you decide what to offer, build out the full picture:

- Stamp Duty Land Tax — use HMRC's calculator for your specific situation.
- Solicitor and conveyancing fees — typically £1,500 to £3,000.
- Survey costs — a full structural survey on older properties costs £600 to £1,500 and is almost always worth it.
- Service charges and ground rent if leasehold — ask for the last three years' accounts.
- Immediate renovation costs — get at least two quotes before making an offer if significant work is needed.
- Ongoing running costs — council tax band, energy efficiency rating, and broadband availability all affect real cost of living.

## Section 5: What to ask at a viewing

A viewing is not just an opportunity to look at the property — it is an opportunity to ask the agent questions they are obliged to answer honestly. Here is a list of questions that experienced buyers use.

### About the property

- What is the tenure — freehold, leasehold, or share of freehold? If leasehold, how many years remain on the lease?
- What are the current service charges and ground rent? Have they increased significantly in recent years?
- When was the boiler last serviced, and how old is it?
- What is the council tax band?
- Has the property ever flooded, or is it in a flood risk area?
- Has the property had any damp, subsidence, or structural issues?
- Are there any known planning applications nearby?
- What is included in the sale — white goods, fixtures, fittings?

### About the sale

- Why is the vendor selling?
- Have they found somewhere to move to, or are they still looking?
- How long has the property been on the market, and have there been any previous offers or sales that fell through?
- What is the vendor's ideal completion timeline?
- Are there any offers already on the table?

### About the neighbours and area

- What are the neighbours like — are any flats above commercial premises, or in HMO use?
- Is there a residents' or management committee for the building, and what has it dealt with recently?
- Are there any ongoing disputes — boundary, party wall, noise?

Agents are legally required to answer questions about the property honestly when asked directly. The key is knowing which questions to ask — and asking them before you are emotionally invested.

## Frequently asked questions

### What should I look for in a property listing as a first-time buyer?

Focus on four things: the tenure (freehold or leasehold), the price relative to recent sold prices in the same postcode, anything the listing is vague or silent about, and any language that appears to soften a negative (words like 'bijou', 'cosy', or 'in need of modernisation'). Read the description with the assumption that every word choice is deliberate.

### How do I know if a house is overpriced?

Look at the price per square foot compared to recent sold prices for similar properties on the same street or in the same postcode. Rightmove's sold prices tool and the Land Registry both give you this data. If the property is priced materially above comparable recent sales and you cannot identify a clear reason why (a recent renovation, significantly better condition, or a particularly desirable plot), it is worth negotiating.

### What does 'offers in excess of' mean?

'Offers in excess of' (OIEO) means the agent is telling you the vendor will not accept less than the listed figure. In practice it is often used to generate multiple offers above that price. It does not mean you cannot offer the listed figure — particularly if the property has been on the market for some time.

### What is the difference between freehold and leasehold?

Freehold means you own the property and the land it stands on outright. Leasehold means you own the right to occupy the property for a fixed term — typically 99 or 125 years — after which ownership reverts to the freeholder. Leasehold properties with fewer than 80 years remaining on the lease become significantly harder to mortgage and can be very expensive to extend. Always check the lease length before proceeding.

### Should I get a survey if I am buying a new build?

Yes, for two reasons. First, a new build snagging survey will identify defects the developer needs to rectify before completion. Second, a HomeBuyer Report will confirm the property has been built to the standard described. Many buyers skip surveys on new builds and later discover issues the developer refuses to take responsibility for.
